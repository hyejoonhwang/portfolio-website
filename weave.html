A Platform for Global Market Entry

A workflow tool that helps companies find, evaluate, contract, and collaborate with overseas partners, all in one flow.

Role

UX Research Lead, Product Strategy, Interaction Design

Timeline

Jul 2025 – Jan 2026

Team

Summer Hwang, Sea Ahn, Jihyeon Jeon

Overview

Why I started this project

I kept hearing the same story from companies trying to expand overseas: weeks lost to scattered tools, uncertain partners, and regulations that seemed to change overnight. The workflow was broken and nobody was connecting the pieces.

I proposed this project concept and led research strategy, problem definition, and solution ideation from initial desk research through feature architecture. Over one semester, we took this from a question to a fully designed product.

End-to-end

Complete workflow from partner discovery to collaboration to compliance

Validated

Tested with 3 users across 2 task flows; iterated on real feedback

Originated

Conceptualized the product vision and led research-driven problem framing

The Problem

Companies expanding globally face fragmented information, resulting in disconnected execution across markets.

Teams over-rely on a small set of local partners, government resources are too broad for real decisions, and critical support is scattered across dozens of institutions. The process feels like assembling a puzzle with pieces from different boxes.

Problem 01

Fragmented Resources

Essential expansion data is scattered across multiple institutions and platforms, costing teams significant time just to locate and consolidate.

Problem 02

No Customized Insights

Existing resources offer broad information, but lack the tailored market analysis companies need for their specific industry and target region.

Problem 03

Partner Dependency & Brand Risk

Heavy reliance on local partners means their actions directly shape the company's brand image, amplifying operational and reputational risk.

Problem 04

Rapidly Changing Regulations

Compliance requirements shift frequently across countries and industries, making them nearly impossible for most teams to track and respond to.

The Solution

A workflow tool that helps companies find overseas partners and collaborate in one connected flow.

Each of the four problems we identified maps to a specific Weave capability, creating a connected system instead of yet another fragmented tool.

Solution 01

Centralized Expansion Hub

One platform aggregating government programs, consulting resources, and market data in a single place.

Solution 02

AI-Powered Roadmap

A tailored expansion plan generated from company profile, industry, and target market.

Solution 03

Partner Marketplace + Co-Management

Vetted partner marketplace with a shared workspace for HQ-partner collaboration.

Solution 04

Regulatory Monitoring & Alerts

Automated tracking of regulation changes with impact analysis and response suggestions.

Key Failure Points

01

Underestimated Regulatory Hurdles

Vietnam requires local bank partnerships and central bank approval — licensing timelines blocked the path to monetization.

02

Misread Cash-Based Culture

Imported the Korean "all-in-one finance" model into a market with low card/account-based payment penetration.

03

No Differentiation

GrabPay, MoMo, and ZaloPay already dominated — Toss couldn't articulate a unique value proposition against entrenched locals.

04

Financial Risk Explosion

Aggressive expansion led to 4x debt growth, forcing leadership to cut overseas operations for domestic IPO focus.

Key to Failure

A strong domestic product doesn't guarantee overseas success. Without understanding local regulations, payment culture, and competitive landscape, growth turns into debt.

Desk Research

What the data told us about overseas expansion

We analyzed government reports, startup failure data, and existing support programs to map the current landscape for Korean companies expanding abroad.

15+

0

Integrated tools built for SME global expansion

Government support exists, but it's scattered

Programs from KOTRA, GoBiz Korea, and KOSME offer real value — but companies either don't know they exist or can't navigate across fragmented institutions.

Partner discovery relies on personal networks

No structured marketplace exists for finding vetted local partners. Companies default to word-of-mouth, increasing risk and limiting options.

Compliance tools serve enterprises, not SMEs

Global legaltech offers advanced regulatory solutions — but they're priced and scoped for large corporations, leaving SMEs with manual, error-prone processes.

Failure patterns are predictable

Across cases, the same gaps recur: underestimating local regulations, lacking market-specific data, and over-relying on a single partner without verification.

Takeaway

Support programs and compliance tools exist, but none are built for SMEs expanding overseas. The gap isn't in capability, it's in accessibility and integration.

Ideation & Process

From problems to a connected solution

Our research revealed a fragmented ecosystem. The challenge was to design a single product that addressed interconnected needs — not just one pain point. We started with a framing question:

"How might we help companies manage global expansion as one connected flow — from regulation to partner to contract to collaboration?"

From there, we structured our approach around three design goals:

Goal 01

Reduce the fragmentation

Bring regulations, partners, contracts, and collaboration into one platform.

Goal 02

Build trust through transparency

Verified partners, tracked compliance, shared visibility across stakeholders.

Goal 03

Make the complex manageable

Structured roadmaps, guided workflows, and AI-assisted suggestions.

We mapped each pain point from our research to a feature area — creating a solution tree that connected user problems to specific product capabilities. This ideation process, which involved translating our Korean-language problem definitions into English design requirements, became the backbone of the product architecture.

AI in Weave

How AI powers the platform

The challenges we identified — scattered regulations, unstructured market data, and fragmented partner information — are exactly the kind of problems AI excels at solving. Manual research across dozens of government databases and institutions takes weeks; AI can continuously crawl, aggregate, and cross-reference these sources in real time. Similarly, matching companies with the right local partners requires processing hundreds of variables (industry fit, regional expertise, compliance history) that are impractical to evaluate by hand. By embedding AI into the workflow rather than treating it as an add-on, Weave transforms raw complexity into structured, actionable guidance at every stage of the expansion process.

Reflection

What I learned from Weave

Scope discipline matters more than feature breadth

With a platform this ambitious, the hardest decisions were about what to leave out. I learned to anchor every feature to a specific user pain point — and cut anything that couldn't trace back to research.

Bilingual design adds real complexity

Translating problem definitions from Korean research into English design requirements wasn't just a language task — it required re-framing concepts for a different cultural context. This experience deepened my appreciation for localization as a design challenge.

Leading research sharpens product intuition

Owning the research from the start — desk research, problem framing, stakeholder synthesis — gave me a direct line from user needs to design decisions. It made every wireframe feel grounded rather than speculative.

What I'd explore next

If I were to continue this project, I'd want to test the roadmap generation flow with real expansion data, explore how AI-powered compliance alerts could adapt to different regulatory environments, and conduct a longer-term study to understand how teams use the collaboration workspace over time.
