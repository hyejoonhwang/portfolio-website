View source

Overview

Find the song that says what you're feeling

Lyrics Search is a Node.js-based music search engine that takes a phrase or sentence as input and finds the song whose lyrics best match what you typed. You don't need to know the title or artist. Just type what you're feeling and the engine finds the track whose lyrics say it for you.

Under the hood, it runs a multi-step pipeline: querying the Genius API for candidates, scraping and parsing full lyrics, scoring each song using a sliding-window matching algorithm, then enriching the best result with Spotify album art and a YouTube video for instant playback. The engine also fetches synced lyrics from LRCLIB to calculate precise timestamps, so the song plays from the exact line that matched your input.

The stack is intentionally lean — Express for the server, SQLite for persistence, Socket.io for real-time delivery, and vanilla HTML/CSS/JS on the frontend. No frameworks, no build tools. The entire client runs in under 500 lines of JavaScript.

How It Works

From text input to a playable song match — here's the pipeline

The search pipeline is designed around accuracy and speed. Your input text passes through four stages, each narrowing down from thousands of potential songs to a single best match with full metadata and a precise playback timestamp. Spotify, YouTube, and LRCLIB calls all run in parallel to keep response times tight.

Design Decisions

Why it works this way

Some of the choices behind how the engine works.

01

Why search by lyrics instead of title or artist?

People remember how a song made them feel, not its metadata. Searching by lyrics lets you find a song from a half-remembered line or even an emotion — the engine translates feelings into music by matching against what the songs actually say.

UX
02

Why a sliding-window scoring algorithm?

A simple keyword search would miss context. The sliding window scans lyrics in 2–4 line chunks, scoring both word overlap and exact substring matches. This catches partial phrases and natural language variations that a naive search would miss entirely.

Engineering
03

Why start playback at the matching line?

If you searched for "I miss you" and the song plays from the beginning, the connection between your input and the result feels lost. Starting at the exact matching lyric creates an immediate "that's it" moment — the proof that the engine understood what you meant.

UX
04

Why run enrichment calls in parallel?

Spotify, YouTube, and LRCLIB are independent data sources — none depends on the other. Running them in parallel with Promise.all cuts the enrichment step from ~3 seconds sequential to under 1 second. Speed matters when the user is waiting for a result.
